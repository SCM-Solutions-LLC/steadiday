2025-2026 Vaccine Updates: What Adults 50+ Need to Know

March 30, 2026 • By SteadiDay Team • 7 min read

If you're wondering whether you need updated vaccines this season, you're not alone. The CDC has just released new vaccination guidelines for 2025-2026, and there are some important changes to be aware of—especially if you're over 50. With experts predicting one of the most challenging flu seasons in decades and concerning drops in vaccination rates among older adults, staying informed about these updates could make a real difference in your health this year.

What's New in the CDC's 2025-2026 COVID-19 Guidelines

The biggest change in this year's COVID-19 vaccination guidance is the shift to what the CDC calls "individual-based decision-making" for people 6 months and older. This means the decision to get vaccinated is now more personalized, taking into account your specific health situation, risk factors, and circumstances.

However, if you're 65 or older, the recommendations remain strong and clear: the CDC continues to emphasize that COVID-19 vaccination is particularly important for your age group. This makes sense when you consider that older adults continue to face higher risks of severe illness, hospitalization, and complications from COVID-19.

What does this mean practically? If you're in your 50s or early 60s, you'll want to have a conversation with your healthcare provider about whether an updated COVID-19 vaccine is right for you, considering factors like your overall health, any chronic conditions, and your exposure risk. If you're 65 or older, the guidance remains straightforward: getting your updated COVID-19 vaccine is recommended.

Why the 2025-2026 Flu Season Has Experts Concerned

Here's something that might surprise you: experts are warning that the 2025-2026 flu season is shaping up to be one of the most severe in 25 years. The reason? A novel H3N2 subclade K strain that's showing up in surveillance data and appears to cause more severe symptoms than we've seen in recent years.

H3N2 strains have historically been associated with more severe flu seasons, particularly impacting older adults. This new subclade K variant seems to be following that pattern, which is why infectious disease specialists are taking it so seriously. The strain appears to be more effective at evading some of our existing immunity, whether from previous infections or vaccinations.

For adults over 50, this is particularly relevant because our immune systems naturally become less robust as we age—a process called immunosenescence. This means we're not only more likely to catch the flu, but we're also more likely to experience severe symptoms, complications, and longer recovery times.

The Concerning Drop in Vaccination Rates

Here's a statistic that should give us all pause: according to recent CDC data from February 2026, flu vaccination rates among older adults dropped from 71% to 67% between 2019 and 2024. Even more concerning, pneumonia vaccination rates fell from 67% to 65% during the same period.

These declining rates come at exactly the wrong time, given the predictions about this flu season's severity. When vaccination rates drop in a community, we lose some of the protective effect that comes from what scientists call "community immunity"—when enough people are vaccinated to help protect those who can't be vaccinated or don't respond as well to vaccines.

Why are rates dropping? Pandemic fatigue might play a role, along with changing attitudes about vaccines and perhaps some confusion about which vaccines are needed when. Whatever the reasons, reversing this trend is crucial for protecting our communities, especially our most vulnerable members.

RSV: The Third Player in This Season's Prevention Strategy

While COVID-19 and flu tend to get most of the attention, RSV (respiratory syncytial virus) deserves a spot in your prevention planning too. RSV isn't just a childhood illness—it can cause serious respiratory problems in adults over 60, leading to hospitalization and complications.

The good news is that we now have effective RSV vaccines for older adults. If you're 60 or older, the CDC recommends discussing RSV vaccination with your healthcare provider. The vaccine has shown excellent effectiveness in preventing severe RSV disease in older adults, and it's typically a one-time vaccination.

RSV season typically overlaps with flu season, running from fall through spring. Having RSV on top of other respiratory illnesses can be particularly challenging for older adults, so prevention through vaccination makes a lot of sense.

Making Vaccination Decisions That Work for You

With all these recommendations, you might feel overwhelmed trying to figure out what you actually need. Here's a practical approach: start by making a list of your current health conditions, medications, and any concerns you have. Then schedule a conversation with your healthcare provider—ideally before peak flu season hits.

Consider your lifestyle too. Do you spend time with grandchildren? Are you a caregiver for someone with health challenges? Do you travel frequently or spend time in crowded places? These factors can influence your risk level and help guide your vaccination decisions.

Remember, vaccines work best when given before you're exposed to illness. For flu vaccines, early fall is typically ideal, but getting vaccinated later in the season still provides protection. COVID-19 vaccines can be timed based on your last vaccination or infection, and RSV vaccines can be given at any time.

Taking Action for a Healthier Season

The key takeaway from all these updates isn't to feel anxious, but to feel empowered. You have tools available—vaccines, preventive measures, and support systems—that can significantly reduce your risk of serious illness this season.

Start by scheduling a conversation with your healthcare provider about which vaccines make sense for your situation. Don't wait until you're in the middle of flu season or dealing with an outbreak. Early fall is the perfect time to get protected and get prepared.

Remember that vaccination is just one part of staying healthy. Continue practicing good hygiene, consider wearing masks in crowded indoor spaces during peak season, stay home when you're feeling unwell, and maintain those social connections that support your overall wellbeing.

This season's challenges are real, but they're not insurmountable. With the right preparation, good information, and a solid support network, you can navigate the 2025-2026 respiratory illness season with confidence. Your health is worth the investment of time and planning it takes to stay protected.
